# DESIGN PROTOCOL: myc3lium 
INTERNAL SPECIFICATION V1.0 

I. THE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY 
myc3lium is at the intersection of brutalist industrialism and biological expansion. The visual identity must reflect a system that is structural yet alive, resilient yet alien. The core aesthetic is "Researcher in the Void"—a sterile, high-stakes environment where hardware meets organic growth in deep-space isolation. 

The Implications 
 * Scale: The brand should feel massive. It is the substrate upon which everything else is built. 
 * Tension: There is a psychological friction between the rigid, heavy lines of the machinery and the branching, unpredictable nature of the mycelium. 
 * Permanence: The design should look like it was stamped into steel or etched into a bulkhead. It is not decorative; it is functional. 

II. THE ANCHOR (THE LOGO) 
The logo is a monolithic "M" that utilizes negative space to house a "3". It is designed to dominate the canvas. 

2.1. Geometric Construction 
 * The M-Frame: A heavy, wide silhouette with aggressive chamfers. It represents the "Host" or the "Base." 
 * The Rotational '3': The '3' is not a separate character. It is a 90-degree clockwise rotation of the 'M' silhouette, scaled and nested into the right side. This implies a recursive, self-referential growth—the system building itself out of its own architecture. 
 * The Whole Canvas Rule: The mark is designed to feel like it is "taking up the whole room." Avoid large, empty margins when the logo is used as a standalone icon. It should feel like it's pushing against the boundaries of the frame. 

2.2. The Scaling Strategy 
 * Macro-Detail: At large sizes, the "3" can contain a subtle, hair-thin branching pattern (the network). 
 * The Micro-Mark: When the logo must exist at a tiny scale (icons, small stamps), discard the "3" entirely. Use only the solid "M" silhouette. The strength of the blocky form allows the brand to remain recognizable even when the secondary detail is lost. 

III. TYPOGRAPHY & COLOR 
The choice of type and color must support the narrative of a technical researcher working in low-light, high-pressure environments. 

3.1. Primary Wordmark: Michroma 
 * Rationale: Michroma is a wide, geometric sans-serif that mirrors the heavy horizontal weight of the logo. It feels industrial, futuristic, and slightly "NASA-spec." 
 * Styling: Used primarily in All-Caps. Increased letter-spacing (tracking) is recommended to ensure legibility on low-resolution digital terminals. 

3.2. Technical & Body Fonts 
 * IBM Plex Sans JP: For technical UI, headers, and buttons. It provides a clean, "IBM-era" computer aesthetic. 
 * Noto Sans JP: For long-form data logs and reports. High legibility is the priority here. 
 * Zen Kaku Gothic Antique: For "Old Data" or archival documents to introduce a subtle sense of historical dread or distance. 

3.3. The Palette 
 * Substrate Brown (#3D3630): The primary mass. Earthy, compressed, and grounded. 
 * Hyphae Orange (#FF6B00): The active element. It represents the "3" identifier and system alerts. It should feel like bioluminescence or high-voltage energy. 
 * The Void (#0A0A0A): Not just "black," but a deep, empty space for the UI to live in. 

IV. THE "DOs" AND "DON'Ts" 
The DOs 
 * DO use aggressive negative space in layouts to simulate isolation. 
 * DO lean into "Micro-Radii." Sharp edges should be softened by only a fraction of a millimeter—just enough to look "manufactured" rather than "digital." 
 * DO treat the orange "3" as a glowing status indicator. It is the "Active" part of the machine. 

The DON'Ts 
 * DON'T use gradients. The brand is built on flat, solid, high-contrast values. 
 * DON'T use thin, elegant typefaces. Everything must have "Weight." 
 * DON'T center-align everything. Use offset, grid-based layouts to mimic a technical dashboard. 
 * DON'T force the "3" nomenclature into standard body text. It is a visual identifier for the logo and specific node names, not a replacement for every "E" in the alphabet. 

V. PSYCHOLOGICAL DIRECTIVE 
The designer should aim for "Unsettling Order." The grid should be perfect, the lines should be straight, but the content (the mycelium) should feel like it is slowly reclaiming the machine. The logo is the barrier holding it all together.
